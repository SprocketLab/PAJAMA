 Looking at the failure patterns, I'm noticing that responses which just restate the question or ask follow-up questions tend to score higher than ones that actually provide substantive answers. The system seems to reward the appearance of structure over genuine problem-solving. There's also an issue with off-topic content and template-like responses getting inflated scores when they shouldn't.

I'm seeing a tension in how the pipeline evaluates length—sometimes it prefers concise, direct answers to specific queries, but other times it favors longer structured responses with Q&A formats or lists, even when the shorter version is correct. Factual accuracy is another major blind spot, like when a wrong numerical answer gets preferred if it's presented in a well-formatted way. And there are clear cases where irrelevant code dumps or garbled text should be penalized but aren't being caught effectively. Looking at these additional cases, I'm seeing clearer patterns emerge. When queries explicitly ask for direct output like "name" or "identify," shorter answers consistently win—Case 26 shows this with the preference for brevity, and Cases 36 and 32 confirm that clean, minimal responses beat padded explanations. Template artifacts like repeated "Instruction:" or "Output:" labels are strong signals of low-quality content, as Case 28 demonstrates. I'm also noticing that when a response is mostly questions rather than answers, especially when the query itself is a question, the pipeline tends to favor it incorrectly—this needs to be penalized. The key insight is that I need to reward directness and penalize structural noise, question-based responses, and garbled formatting.
